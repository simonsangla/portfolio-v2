## Problem

Every Monday, five product teams across the TripAdvisor group met to review the previous week's KPIs. Before those meetings, a rotating analyst spent the better part of a week assembling the same artifact: a deck of charts, bullet points, and tentative causal claims about why bookings, conversion, or repeat rate had moved.

The work was slow, and worse, it was unreliable. By the time the analyst had decomposed the headline metric by cohort, channel, and platform, the analyst-hours were spent and the meeting had started. Root causes were often missed. Early-warning signals — drops in a precursor metric that would predict a booking dip two weeks out — were ignored because nobody had time to look at them. Clustering signals across surfaces were invisible to any single analyst.

The teams kept making decisions. The decisions kept being half-informed. The cost compounded.

## Approach

I built a system — internally called the **Business Metric Wizard** — that automated the weekly diagnostic end-to-end. The structural intuition was simple: the work humans were doing by hand was mostly mechanical. Decompose the metric, detect the change-point, describe the result in plain English. Machines could do all three, and do them more consistently than an analyst working against a deadline.

Three components, glued together:

1. **Additive KPI decomposition** — break the headline metric into its additive drivers (volume × conversion × AOV × return rate, applied at the cohort level). Rank drivers by absolute contribution to the week-over-week delta.
2. **Statistical change-point detection** — Theil–Sen slope estimators plus STL seasonal-trend decomposition to isolate *when* and *where* a driver broke, with cohort / channel / platform / geography as the slicers.
3. **Natural-language summarization** — a template layer that turned the ranked driver list plus change-point output into a short written brief: "Repeat-guest bookings are down 4.3%. 82% of the drop is concentrated in mobile-web in FR + IT, starting week of 2020-W14."

All of it ran nightly inside the Snowflake warehouse. Zero data left the perimeter. The LLM summarization layer (added in 2022) ran on a locally-hosted model.

## Stack

- **Warehouse:** Snowflake (event + orders layer modeled in dbt)
- **Transforms:** Python (`pandas`, `statsmodels`) + `STL` decomposition
- **Change-point:** Theil–Sen slope + CUSUM for narrow windows
- **Orchestration:** Airflow, nightly DAG
- **Delivery:** auto-generated Notion pages per team, plus a slim Streamlit explorer for the driver drill-down
- **Summarization layer:** locally-hosted LLM (no vendor round-trip on production data)

## Metrics before

- Average analyst-hours spent per weekly review across the five teams: **~40 hours/week** (roughly one senior analyst-week).
- Root-cause diagnosis: analyst-led, inconsistent across teams, often contradicted week-over-week.
- Precursor-metric surveillance: **zero** — no team was monitoring lead indicators.
- Cross-team clustering: invisible. Two teams seeing the same driver break never found out until an ad-hoc cross-org review.

## Metrics after

- Analyst-hours per weekly review: **~8 hours/week**, used for interpreting the auto-brief rather than building it.
- Investigation time: **−80%**. The diagnostic was ready by 06:00 Monday.
- Precursor surveillance went from zero to a standing weekly briefing across all five teams.
- Downstream: the system seeded a self-service Insight Bot that grew to **500+ queries/month** across the org, still in use.
- The Wizard ran in production for ten years and survived a full warehouse migration.

## What I built

- The decomposition + change-point library (internal).
- The summarization templating layer + evaluation set.
- The Notion + Slack delivery layer.
- A training rollout: two hour-long workshops per team on how to read the auto-brief and when to override it.
- An escalation path: if the brief flagged a cross-team cluster, it automatically notified the relevant team leads.

## What I'd do differently

- **Ship the summarization layer sooner.** The first two years were decomposition-only; teams had to interpret a table of drivers. Once the natural-language layer landed, adoption jumped. I should have treated the text output as the product from week one.
- **Build the explorer before the batch pipeline.** I led with the nightly run and bolted on a self-serve layer later; the reverse would have let analysts pull ad-hoc questions into the system faster.
- **Write the evaluation set earlier.** We had qualitative "the brief feels right" for too long before I built a ground-truth set of historical incidents to regression-test against.

## Lessons

- **Structural problems beat heroic workflows.** The analyst-week wasn't a staffing problem; it was a tooling gap. Any team with the same diagnostic cadence and the same warehouse shape is paying the same cost.
- **Determinism earns trust.** The moment the auto-brief produced one confidently wrong answer, the team stopped trusting every brief. Tight evaluation, narrow confidence claims, and a "show the decomposition underneath" escape hatch are non-negotiable.
- **Local models clear the blast-radius conversation in one sentence.** "No data leaves the warehouse" made the security review ten minutes instead of ten weeks.
- **The engine is portable; the organizational pattern is not.** The Wizard survived at TripAdvisor because the weekly-review ritual existed and had teeth. Deploying the same engine into a team without that ritual would have been a museum piece.

This is the engine that MetricPilot now ships as a fixed-scope engagement for B2C scale-ups on Snowflake. Same decomposition, same change-point detection, same local-only summarization — productized, documented, and handed off to the client's team at day 28.
